islands of coherence

"when a complex system is far from equilibrium, small islands of coherence in a sea of chaos have the capacity to shift the entire system." — attributed to ilya prigogine

somewhere, a community already solved it. for every problem that is local but exists everywhere — homelessness, addiction, trash — some town figured out an answer that actually worked, and almost nobody copied it. these are those answers, documented as protocols: instruction sets concrete enough that a group of neighbors could run them.

01 · rehabilitation & reentry · san francisco · since 1971

🏗️ delancey street foundation

delancey street takes the population every other program screens out — addiction, multiple violent felonies, generations of family incarceration — and it has no professional staff. every member, all the way up to the leadership, is a former offender. residents commit to two-plus years, live together, and run the businesses that fund the place: a moving company, a restaurant, retail. the operating principle is each one teach one — you arrive, someone slightly ahead of you teaches you; within months you're teaching someone newer. being needed is the treatment.

~95% lasting rehabilitation reported · 50+ years running · 0 treatment staff

the protocol

  • recruit from the hardest population — don't screen for the easiest
  • no staff/client divide: the people being rehabilitated run the organization, leadership included
  • multi-year residential commitment — long enough to rebuild identity, not just behavior
  • each one teach one: every resident teaches the person behind them
  • fund through resident-run businesses, so the model earns its independence
  • graduate into leadership: the c-suite came through the program

the 95% figure is as reported — formal studies are limited; verify before citing. via the case study library ↗

02 · homelessness · brasília, brazilstory unverified

🧹 paying the homeless to clean the city

as the story goes: around 2007, a civil-engineer mayor stopped treating trash and homelessness as separate line items and collapsed them into one program — the city paid homeless residents to collect trash and paint over graffiti. the results stacked: clean streets, income for the people who needed it most, rising property values — and it cost less than the separate programs it replaced. almost no other city has copied it.

two problems, one program · city budget went down · visible homelessness ~0 (reported)

the protocol

  • find two chronic problems where one population's need (income) is the other problem's missing workforce
  • pay directly and simply: cash for visible public work — skip the program-industrial complex
  • price it against the status quo: cheaper than the separate spend it replaces
  • measure everything — cleanliness, homelessness, property values, total budget — so the win is undeniable
  • publish the numbers so other cities can copy it

told from memory and still being sourced — it closely resembles curitiba under jaime lerner, so city, date, and details may shift. via the case study library ↗
